The history of anti-new movements—spanning philosophy, science, technology, 
and innovation—is a long-standing pattern of resistance driven by economic distress, cultural shifts, or existential fear. 
Historically, these movements are often mischaracterized as purely "anti-technology," when they frequently represent social or labor efforts to manage the 
disruptive impacts of new methods.

Key Historical Anti-Technology Movements
The Luddites (1811–1816): Skilled English textile workers (weavers, stockingers) who targeted automated machinery during the Napoleonic Wars. They were not 
inherently anti-tech, but rather protested the use of machinery to circumvent fair labor practices, lower wages, and produce inferior goods. 
Their "machine-breaking" was a form of collective bargaining by riot.Swing Riots (1830s): Agricultural laborers in southern England destroyed threshing machines, 
which they viewed as the cause of their unemployment and poverty.
Romantic Movement (Late 18th–19th Century): Emerging as a reaction against the industrial revolution's pollution, urban poverty, and the perceived "ugliness" of 
industrial cities, Romantics—including thinkers like Lord Byron and Victor Hugo—emphasized emotion over reason, countering the technocratic progress of the Age of 
Reason.
Arts and Crafts Movement (Late 19th Century): Opposed the dehumanizing, industrial manufacturing of the Victorian era, advocating for traditional craftsmanship and 
locally produced, high-quality goods.

Modern "Anti-New" Movements (Neo-Luddism)
Neo-Luddism emerged in the late 20th century, broader in scope than its predecessor, often questioning the very premise that technology represents progress.

Neo-Luddites (1970s–Present): A diverse, often decentralized movement comprising environmentalists, "back-to-the-land" advocates, and critics of computer age technology. They advocate for simple living, the precautionary principle (proving safety before adopting technology), and sometimes sabotage.
Back-to-the-land Movements (1960s-70s): Aimed to return to simpler, pre-industrial lifestyles.
Anti-Nuclear Movements (1960s–80s): Peaked in the late 1970s following events like Three Mile Island, reaching a peak in 1982 when one million people marched in New York City against nuclear proliferation.

Anti-Science and Anti-Innovation Movements
Anti-Science: Active rejection of scientific methods or findings, often driven by cultural or ideological conflicts rather than economic ones.
Anti-Innovation League: A contemporary term for political/social forces that stifle new technologies, such as Artificial Intelligence, through regulatory, ideological, or legal opposition.
Environmentalism/Deep Ecology: Some factions within these movements are critical of technocratic solutions to ecological problems, arguing they create further, deeper problems.

Core Themes and Motivations
Dehumanization: Resistance to machines that replace human craftsmanship or reduce workers to mere components of a "system".
Labor and Wages: Fear of job displacement by automation and the reduction of workers' bargaining power.
Environmental Degradation: Distrust of technologies perceived to damage nature, such as nuclear energy, genetically modified organisms (GMOs), or chemical technologies.
Environmental/Cultural Loss: Opposition to technologies that dismantle established communities or threaten natural beauty, as seen in the Romantic Movement.
Opposition to Centralized Control: Resistance against surveillance technologies or big tech monopolies that concentrate power.

Evolution of "Anti-New" Resistance
Sabotage to Criticism: Early movements used physical destruction of machinery (sabotage). Modern movements, while sometimes resorting to extreme measures (e.g., the Unabomber or anarchist attacks), often use legal regulation, activism, and "degrowth" philosophy to fight technology.
Digital Luddites: Modern critics of Big Tech, such as the Algorithmic Justice League, who use digital tools and lobbying to protest AI bias, surveillance capitalism, and the erosion of privacy.
"Innovation" as a Dirty Word: Historically, "innovation" was a pejorative term from the 15th to 17th centuries, used to accuse religious and political rebels of spreading heresy and breaking with tradition.
